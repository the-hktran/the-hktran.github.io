Quantum Embedding



Advanced electronic structure methods scale steeply with system size, limiting their applicability on real systems. Electron correlation is intrinsically local and this can be exploited by a variety of embedding methods. These embedding methods partition the system into small fragments that can each be manageably treated with high-accuracy theories, and use a cheaper theory for the interactions of the fragment with its surrounding bath. This different treatment of subsystems appears in the fragment-bath interface, where the fragment and bath interactions are the most erroneous. Various flavors of embedding differ in the choice of theory for the bath and how this interaction is corrected.

In our work, we have focused on embedding methods based upon the Schmidt decomposition. After defining a fragment, bath states that contain all of the coherence with the fragment states, can be obtained via the Schmidt decomposition. Formally, bath states obtained from the Schmidt decomposition allow for an exact determination of the fragment-bath interactions. However, for practical calculations, coherence can only be maximized at the mean-field level of theory.

We have developed and implemented many methods to improve the fragment-bath interactions through the use of self-consistency conditions. In particular, we have helped drive innovations in density matrix embedding theory (DMET) and bootstrap embedding (BE). In future works, we hope to elevate these methods beyond model systems for applications to real molecules and materials as well as extend these successes to the realm of vibrational structure.

Vibrational CI

The vibrational problem is a long-standing problem with many applications in identifying infra-red spectra and phonons in materials. The methodology in place has allowed for accurate predictions at the dawn of quantum mechanics. Since then, greater experimental tools and a greater understanding of quantum systems demand more accurate and better scaling vibrational structure methods.

Electronic structure methods have seen massive developments in the past few decades. The vibrational structure problem can be reframed in similar methods, but is difficult because the exact potential is unknown and most approximations treat the potential as very high order many-body interactions. This new frontier of vibrational structure has much to be explored. We are seeking to see the same improvement in vibrational structure that advances in electronic structure has helped that field. We are also looking into incorporating non-Born Oppenheimer effects with the ultimate goal of studying electron-phonon interactions in materials and developing frameworks for large-amplitude motions.

Spectroscopy



Spectroscopy has wide-ranging applications in physical sciences from quantitative analysis to component identification. Interpreting spectra serves to translate experimental results and reveals weaknesses in theories when they fail to explain spectra. We are broadly interested in infra-red and X-ray spectroscopy, which are important for analytical and mechanistic studies.
